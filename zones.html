Dividing Intensity Zones By Breath

I have adapted my definition of zones from reading Gordo Byrn's Endurance Essentials.

Background

There is not much consensus on training intensity zones. Common approaches seem to use heart rate, blood lactate concentration, or breathing effort.

I believe breathing effort to be the most useful way to determine zones. The zones are defined bottom-up, which is also how fitness is built. It does not depend on access to a heart rate monitor or lactate testing. That being said:

  • My system, despite being defined by breath markers only, is originally derived from blood lactate concentration.
  • Combining breathing effort with heart rate is a great way of making sure you are not going too hard. See "knowing your zone-wise heart rates".
Remember, this is all rough, but useful. Good enough is good enough.

8-Zone System: The Full Range Of Intensities

I learnt this from Gordo Byrn's e-book. He credits its development to Alan Couzens.
  • Zone 0: Feels like you feel while reading this. You may as well not be doing anything. Walking slowly and talking on the phone.

  • Zone 1: You are working, but breathing does not change compared to Zone 0. Walking strong, or jogging veeery slowly but still talking on the phone.
  • Zone 2: "First deepening of breath". Subtle pause in breathing. While talking on the phone, an uphill appears. Or, you are in a bit of a hurry so you briskly walk, or jog slowly because you cannot run in your current shoes.
  • Zone 3: Can hear yourself breathing, but it's still comfortable and you are not panting. The jog has turned into a run, but you can still hold a conversation. Slight pause between full sentences.
  • Zone 4: Panting begins. Conversation possible but you prefer not to. This is "comfortably hard" and what we expect exercise to feel like.
  • Zone 5: Only one or a few words at a time. Clock is ticking. Roughly from 1 mile race effort to 10km race effort.

  • Zone 6: Too fast to be called endurance training. Mostly anaerobic in nature. 20 to 30 second-long strides are done at this effort.
  • Zone 7: Short fast bursts close to max effort. 10-second sprints are done in this zone.

5-Zone System: Endurance Zones

I learnt this from Gordo Byrn's e-book. He credits its development to John Hellemans.

We focus only on Zones 1 to 5 from the above section. Home to all of our endurance training.

  • Important note: Training max heart rate is lower than race max heart rate. Max heart rate in cold weather is less than in hot weather.

  • Zone 1: easy. More than 50bpm less than max heart rate.
  • Zone 2: steady. 30 to 50bpm below max heart rate.
  • Zone 3: mod-hard. 20 to 40bpm below max heart rate.
  • Zone 4: hard. 10 to 30 beats below max heart rate.
  • Zone 5: very hard. Within 10bpm of max heart rate

  • The heart rate guides are only rough. Do a field test and gather your own data.

3-Zone System (Domains): Best Way To Track Training

I learnt this from Gordo Byrn's e-book. It is a simplification of the 5-zone system that is most useful for tracking your training.
  • Zone 1 and Zone 2 = green zone OR moderate domain
  • Zone 3 and Zone 4 = orange zone OR heavy domain
  • Zone 5 = red zone OR severe domain

  • This system works best for training tracking purposes because:
  • Almost all training sessions fall into 3 types.
    • Endurance sessions are anchored at the Zone 1/Zone 2 border.
    • Threshold sessions are anchored at the Zone 3/Zone 4 border.
    • VO2Max sessions are in Zone 5.
  • Exact definition of zone boundaries in 5-zone system is still too rough. But nothing occurs at zone boundaries in the 3-zone model.
  • For example, we hardly train at the moderate domain/heavy domain border, so it is easy to know whether you were in the moderate domain or heavy domain.
